OVERVIEW

Traveling with your family or a group of friends, can become one of the most amazing times of our lives, where we bond over experiences, inside jokes and reminisce about the trips for years to come. Other times, well...can be a time left forgotten. TravelPak is a travel planning web and mobile application designed to help group travelers plan, manage and organize your trips, making group travel planning easier than ever.

THE CHALLENGE

Planning trips with friends is difficult. Typically involves more research, patience and a lot of organization, which can lead to tension, indecisiveness and lack of communication. Whether you are traveling with a friend or a huge group, balancing each other’s preferences and schedules, can become one of the most frustrating aspects of planning.

The inspiration for creating TravelPak originates with my own personal experience planning trips with a group of friends. Although, most trips were memorable and successful, the early planning stages of any trip will always come with its own set of constraints. From balancing different personalities, researching between multiple tabs, waiting for feedback from others to creating a spreadsheet that half of the group never opens.

THE SOLUTION

TravelPak is an intuitive multi-user planning application that is changing the way travelers plan their group trips. The goal is to alleviate the stress and pain of planning by streamlining the process with each trip’s centralized editable doc that’s sharable amongst friends. TravelPak includes smart travel planning tools, that not only gives the user the confidence of planning but an unforgettable experience that also sets clear expectations for each traveler.

RESEARCH & DISCOVERY

Competitive Analysis

To begin tackling any assumptions that I may have regarding travel, it was important to get a sense of what’s currently available in today’s market by researching what’s working well for the competition and identify where the competition is falling short. It was surprising to learn there aren’t many travel planning applications that solved a suite of every single problem but many applications that solve very specific problems. Ultimately, a competitive analysis was conducted on Travefy, Google Trips and Travelchime. Below are some highlighted pros and cons.

  • Ability to personalize cover photo for each trip

  • In-app messaging

  • Mini instructions to guide new users

  • Not for general travel planning use

  • Information overload on main dashboard

  • Huge database with strong search capabilities

  • Integration with other Google Applications

  • Auto imports reservations from user’s gmail account

  • For single person use

  • Mobile Only

  • Real-time feedback by liking an entry add to the itinerary

  • Multi-user editable doc, collaborate with friends

  • Integrated with Google Maps

  • Web application only

  • Very early stage (launched in March 2019)

VIEW COMP ANALYSIS
Takeaways

With these findings, it was important to include the following features and processes that make these competitors successful:

  • Travefy’s main dashboard is simple and straightforward, with mini blurbs of instructions for a smooth onboarding experience

  • Google Trips’ ability to auto import reservations pulled from the user’s gmail and vivid imagery specific to each trip.

  • TravelChime’s multi-user editable doc, which encourages more collaboration and ease of getting feedback from co-travelers

User Personas

After gathering information on competitors and analyzing the results from the user survey, I conducted follow-up user interviews with potential users that answered the user survey. The goal for the user interviews was to delve into their responses and better understand their motivations and attitudes towards the problem and potential solution. With these conversations and the earlier information gathered, resulted in three different personas that represents the target users for TravelPak.

SARA

Cultural Planner

  • Age: 27

  • Occupation: 6th Grade Teacher

  • Location: Portland, OR

  • Motivations: Sara believes traveling is a humbling experience, where she’s able to witness the different lifestyles around the world. She understands when traveling with others, can be frustrating but as long as everyone is happy and having a good time, that’s all that matters.

Adventure Seeker

Low Fidelity Wireframes + Testing

Taking the user flows, I began sketching my initial screens on Adobe Sketch then used Sketch to create a set of wireframes for testing. With my lofi wireframes ready for testing, I began the first round of usability testing through a series of tasks in maze and 2 in-person usability testing using the compiled lofi wireframes into Invision. I wanted to test users to validate my design decisions and if certain user flows made sense. I asked users to perform the following tasks:

Task #1: Create an account and go through the onboarding process. I wanted to test how the user interacted with the design and if the onboarding experience was smooth.

Task #2: Invite a friend to the trip, I wanted to find out if this flow was straightforward and intuitive.

Task #3: Create a new checklist, I wanted to see if this task reinforces the need for this feature and the ease of creating a list.

Task #4: I wanted to test if users knew what timeline meant.

Takeaways

From the first intial user testing with the lofi mobile wireframes, I learned through casual conversations that most particpants preferred planning their trips on desktop. To validate if this was true, a second survey was sent out to confirm the following, which device do users prefer to use to plan their trips. After collecting 32 responses, the results confirmed:

84%

of respondents prefer desktop for planning a trip

After putting together the lofi desktop wireframes, I was confident to conduct the user testing. I learned many users preferred to enter their email address to automatically import their initial reservations to begin their trip planning, less work = less data entry. For the onboarding experience, users felt it was straightforward and easy. With the other tasks, there was some confusion and long pauses, users were thinking about the next steps to accomplish the next task. With the feedback, I learned to make the following changes for the next round of iterations:

  • The label, Ideas, was changed to suggestions

  • The label, Timeline, was changed to Itinerary

  • Remove the lines connected the icons in the itinerary, users felt it was the exact order users must follow

  • Lack of specific days, users are unsure which day the itinerary is showing

VISUAL DESIGN & TESTING

High Fidelity Mockups

Together with the style guide and feedback from the first round of testing, several design iterations were made and the initial set of HiFi mockups were developed. Below are examples of some iterations based on the feedback I received for the first round of testing:

In the lofi wireframes, users were tasked to go through the onboarding process. Most users pointed out how the layout of the trips dashboard felt “mobile” like and did not utilize the page real estate.

With the hifi mockups, the tabs - Upcoming, Past and Recommendations are now organized and sectioned on the trips dashboard, for quicker navigation, while using the page real estate.

In the lofi wireframes, users felt the lines connecting the icons meant this was the exact order of events for Saturday. Also, the labeling, Timeline caused some confusion.

With the hifi mockups, the label, timeline was changed to itinerary. This addresses the vocabulary confusion. Also, the lines were removed from the icons.

Usability Testing: Round 2

After adding the visual aesthetics and applying the feedback received earlier, the second round of user testing commenced. Five in-person user testing were conducted and Maze was utilized, to ensure the design elements reinforced the brand identity and the overall cohesion of the visual design and usability of the product. Users were asked the following:

Task #1: Add a new restaurant to the itinerary. I wanted to test one of the main fuctions of creating a trip, adding a reservation to an itinerary.

Task #2: Save an activity and creating a new saved list to save the activity. I wanted to test if this task was intuitive and if this flow is what a user would conduct.

Task #3: Add the saved activity into their itinerary. I wanted to test the user flow and users knew how to complete this task.

Using the feedback, I identified any common patterns and with those patterns, I applied those design changes as a continued iteration for a better user experience. Below is an example of an iteration based on the secound round of feedback:

Initial Hi-Fi mockup of adding an activity from saved list

Final mockup of adding an activity from saved list

Takeaways

With the initial hifi mockups, users relayed all tasks were straightforward and easy to follow. From the example above, users felt when adding a saved activity to their itinerary, the green circle checkmark was abrupt and was not a smooth transition. The transition went from a rectangular shape to a circle. Thus, I kept the original shape of the button and color green but added the labeling, “Added to Itinerary”, plus checkmark icon.

FINAL PROTOTYPE

After the second round of user testing and several iterations later, the final prototype of TravelPak was put together. TravelPak currently contains a number of features that improves and enhancing the planning stages of any trip. Some highlighted features:

Centralized Platform

Don’t know where to start with planning? TravelPak simply gathers travel information from user’s email inbox, then organizes it automatically. View and store all reservations, saved research and so much more on one simple platform.

In-App Messaging

Want to know if everyone booked their flights? Want to say hello? TravelPak has made it easier to connect and communicate with anyone in the travel group.

Real-Time Feedback

Want to know if friends want to go skydiving on Friday? Get real-time feedback with TravelPak’s social and interactive features by liking an activity or adding a note.

REFLECTIONS

Entering into the digital travel planning space hosts a lot of opportunities but comes with its own set of limitations. While TravelPak only scratches the surface, piecing together this concept project was a great learning experience from testing many assumptions to conjuring up a possible solution.

LESSONS LEARNED

One of the biggest lessons I learned was to ask questions early on in the research and discovery phase. As mentioned above, if I had asked which device users preferred to use while planning, I would not have only iterated a mobile version of the app. This obvious question could’ve saved me a couple of days but this oversight made me experience the design process in true form, going back to a previous step.

CONCLUSION

While creating a travel planning application is ambitious and a useful concept, there is a vast amount of other considerations to think about, besides which features should be included and how visually everything should look. While completing the competitive analysis, I was shocked there was not an established web application made specifically for group travel planning. I found many specific tools for planning a trip but nothing that combined everything, such as, curated content with strong search capabilities, using AI for travel inspirations, importing all travel photos, collaboration features, you name it. I soon realized, there are many technical factors to consider, starting with how to consider users that have different traveling styles, social economical reasons, personalities, and so on.

Looking past these considerations, TravelPak received great initial feedback from potential users. It reinforces that TravelPak is heading in the right direction as a better solution to group travel planning.
